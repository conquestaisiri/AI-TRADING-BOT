Because most people build “trading bots” backwards.

The smarter way is to build this like a decision system with layers:

Data layer – collect market data cleanly

Signal layer – detect real opportunities

Risk layer – decide position size, stop, invalidation

Execution layer – place/manage orders

AI layer – interpret, rank, explain, adapt, and refuse bad trades

Learning layer – review wins/losses and improve rules over time

And yes, we can do this using free or open models, plus exchange APIs and open-source trading frameworks. Open-source model options with strong current relevance include Meta’s Llama 3.x family, Qwen3 variants, and DeepSeek reasoning/distilled models; for backtesting/research, vectorbt is actively maintained and designed for fast quantitative analysis; and exchanges like Binance, Coinbase Exchange, and Kraken provide official APIs for market access.

Have one AI argue for the trade and another argue against it.

3. Self-journaling edge discovery

5. Regime-switching architecture
Instead of one static strategy, let the system switch playbooks:

trend strategy in strong directional market

mean reversion in range

no-trade in chaos

This is more realistic than pretending one strategy wins everywhere.

6. Confidence-weighted simulation
Don’t just ask “did the trade win?”

Ask:

when AI confidence was 8+, was expectancy better?

when AI confidence disagreed with rules, what happened?

when risk officer flagged caution, did trades underperform?

That tells you whether AI is adding actual value or just sounding smart.
